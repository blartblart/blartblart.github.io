1.2 Network Sovereignty – Intro, CH 1 & 2

Dr. Yellow Horse Brave Heart pictured above.

“Dr. Yellow Horse Brave Heart’s point cannot be understated: to work against colonialism is not simply a matter of protesting the World Trade Organizations, building schools in far-off Indigenous locales, and practicing policy reform. One of the most nefarious dimensions of colonization is how it takes root in the psyche, limiting intellectual vision and poisoning the spirit with defeat by white supremacy.” page 30.

This quote really hit home because this reading, particularly chapter 1 took me through some examinations of colonialism which I had never experienced.

I grew up in rural New Mexico in a politically left leaning family. As a traveling adult, I’ve come to realize that this upbringing gave me an above average exposure to ideas surrounding colonization. That being said, I’ve never taken it upon myself to examine literature put out by indigenous scholars on the topic.

One of the most striking points in the reading was for me when the 4 mechanisms for Spanish colonialism were explained. The fourth mechanism, epistemological redistribution, I think is the mechanism which Dr. Yellow Horse Brave Heart’s point is most heavily referencing. This mechanism is what perpetuates the other three mechanisms, and they all strengthen each other in a reinforcing feedback loop. I think this 4th mechanism is an extremely significant catalyst in perpetuating that cycle.

It is this mechanism which has I think led to the delay in my exposure to such literature, despite my engrained awareness that such violent societal histories and current consequences exist. Even if you come from a politically left leaning family, there is white supremacy engrained. It’s staggering to realize how my brain has for so much of my life labeled nonwhite people as “other”. It’s so seemingly normalized. Because other louder obvious forms of racism exist, the examination of one’s own prejudices is easy to pass by. It’s a pretty vicious cycle to observe and gives me an even greater reverence for the tedious, painful, patient work of Indigenous scholars and activists.

1.2 Network Sovereignty – CH 3, 4 & 5

Pictured above, Washington state overlayed with tribal territories according to the Whose Land app.

“Thus in the case of tribal broadband deployment, the idea of place-based ways of knowing refers to not only ecological knowledge and tribal philosophies but also knowledge of the local political, social, economic, and technical terrain. At present there are more the 568 federally recognized tribes within US boarders. This does not account for tribes that are state-recognized and those that are unrecognized by federal and state governments yet bear the rights of inherently sovereign Native peoples. In the years to come, each of these communities – tribes and peoples – will apply broadband access solutions according to its technical capacity, unique landscapes, and needs. There should be more than 568 solutions to acquiring broadband access in Indian Country.”

This paragraph addressing the heart of the problem with living in a highly dynamic and networked society. Reductive modeling has induced leaps of technological progress over centuries. In those reductions, costs for such progress were measured reductively and according to convenience of those in power.

The author propositions their readers in this paragraph to engage with complexity in problem solving. In my opinion, this a tactic to propel us toward social progress. It’s painstaking and goes against the status quo to propose more than 568 solutions to attaining broadband access on tribal land. This however is necessary due to the glaring harm reductionist tactics have rippled through society for generations.

This proposition also urges readers to consider each tribe truly as a sovereign nation. I think something I can certainly take away from this reading in my personal views is that understanding that I had been taking on a reductionist view of rolling all the indigenous peoples within the US borders into one category in my mind. The institution of federal government stood as one entity, and the indigenous tribes have been standing as one entity, and those two would be inherently in oppositional dualism. In reading about the layers of sovereignty of each tribe, a multitude of new vectors have been revealed.
